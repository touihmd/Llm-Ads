## 2. The LLM-ADS Vision

### 2.1 Definition

LLM-ADS is a contextual mediation protocol: a set of rules, interfaces, and governance mechanisms enabling commercial entities â€” businesses, experts, institutions, startups â€” to distribute relevant content to LLM users, within a dedicated window separated from conversational space, based on explicit user consent and certified content quality.

### 2.2 The Five Pillars

**01 â€” SEPARATION**
The contextual window is architecturally distinct from conversation space. No commercial content can appear in the model's responses themselves.

**02 â€” CONSENT**
The user explicitly chooses to activate the contextual window, defines initial areas of interest, and can deactivate the service at any time.

**03 â€” QUALITY**
Content ranking is determined primarily by a Quality Score calculated by an independent third-party body â€” not by bid amount alone.

**04 â€” TRANSPARENCY**
Each content element displayed is clearly identified as such, with indication of source, quality score, and contextual display reason.

**05 â€” RELEVANCE**
The system selects content not based on sensitive personal data, but on declared interests and session contextual intent.

### 2.3 What LLM-ADS Is Not

- Not a system of behavioral surveillance
- Not advertising injected into model responses
- Not a mechanism for opinion or choice manipulation
- Not reserved for companies with massive advertising budgets
- Not a proprietary platform belonging to a single operator

## 3. Technical Architecture

### 3.1 The Four Layers

**Layer 1: User Interface**
The contextual window displayed in the LLM interface, clearly visually delimited, containing qualified content results. This layer is under full control of the LLM operator and must respect the UX standards defined by the protocol.

**Layer 2: Contextual Relevance Engine**
A real-time conversational intent analysis module that identifies session thematic domains (without access to sensitive personal data), and submits a query to the content registry.

**Layer 3: Certified Content Registry**
The centralized database of advertiser-submitted content, annotated with third-party quality scores, indexed by thematic domain, geography, and language.

**Layer 4: AQIA (Governance Body)**
The Authority for Quality in AI Advertising â€” the independent entity that certifies content, calculates scores, arbitrates disputes, and annually revises criteria.

### 3.2 Operational Flow

1. User initiates conversation with LLM
2. Contextual Relevance Engine analyzes session themes from declared content and pre-registered interests
3. Anonymized query sent to Certified Content Registry
4. Registry returns content selection ranked by quality score and contextual relevance
5. Content displays in dedicated window, clearly separated from conversational response
6. User can freely interact with content, ignore it, or refine preferences

### 3.3 Open Technical Standards

The protocol defines open, standardized APIs enabling any LLM operator to implement it regardless of technical infrastructure. These standards are deposited with an international standardization body (W3C or ISO) to guarantee their non-exclusive character and evolvability.

## 4. The Personalized Contextual Window

### 4.1 Concept and Design

The personalized contextual window is the central element of the user experience in LLM-ADS. It constitutes a distinct space, visually separated from the conversational interface, accessible only to users who have subscribed to a paid plan (Plus, Pro, or Enterprise) and have explicitly activated the functionality.

This window is never intrusive. It does not impose itself on the user in the middle of a response. It occupies a peripheral or complementary space in the interface, whose precise design is left to the LLM operator's discretion within the protocol's UX guidelines.

### 4.2 Initialization and Preferences

At initial activation, the user follows a simple onboarding process allowing selection of general interest domains: technology, health, education, finance, travel, food, art, sport, environment, and other protocol-defined categories. These preferences constitute "white data" â€” aggregated and anonymized signals that reveal nothing of individual privacy but enable significant personalization.

Beyond these initial preferences, the system progressively refines its selection based on the session's conversational themes. If a user discusses sports nutrition with the LLM, the contextual window adapts its content accordingly â€” without ever storing or transmitting the conversation content itself.

**The Key Analogy:**
> It is the same principle as choosing interests when signing up for a social network â€” with the fundamental difference that it is the AI that intelligently refines this selection in real time, based on session context, and not a behavioral profile accumulated over years.

### 4.3 The 360Â° Optimization Cycle

The protocol introduces the "360Â° Cycle" concept: a continuous improvement loop in which displayed content influences user interactions, which in turn enrich the system's contextual understanding, which improves the relevance of subsequent content. This cycle is entirely anonymized and creates no persistent individual profile.

## 5. Governance and the AQIA

### 5.1 The Authority for Quality in AI Advertising (AQIA)

The cornerstone of the LLM-ADS protocol is the existence of an independent governance body: the Authority for Quality in AI Advertising (AQIA). This body plays a role analogous to rating agencies in finance, or accreditation bodies in academia: it establishes quality standards, evaluates submitted content, and certifies compliance.

### 5.2 AQIA Structure

The AQIA consists of representatives from the principal ecosystem stakeholders, structured in distinct colleges to guarantee power balance:

- **Operators College**: Representatives of LLM platforms participating in the protocol
- **Advertisers College**: Company representatives â€” SMEs and large accounts combined
- **Civil Society College**: Consumer defense organizations and data protection experts
- **Academic College**: AI, digital ethics, and attention economy researchers
- **Regulators College**: Observers from competent regulatory authorities (CNIL, ICO, FTC, etc.)

### 5.3 Annual Revision Mechanism

Quality criteria and score weightings are revised annually by the AQIA through a public, documented process incorporating:
- Global geopolitical developments (new regulations, cultural sensitivities, social priorities)
- Technological advances (new LLM capabilities, new abuse risks)
- User and advertiser feedback
- Global advertising market trends

### 5.4 Certified Third-Party Evaluators

To guarantee system scalability, the AQIA can accredit Certified Third-Party Evaluators (CTEs) â€” organizations specialized in digital content auditing, modeled on Trustpilot, G2, or sectoral quality certification bodies. CTEs operate under AQIA-defined standards, are regularly audited, and cannot have capital ties with the advertisers they evaluate.

## 6. Quality Model and Pricing

### 6.1 The LLM-ADS Quality Score (SQL)

The LLM-ADS Quality Score (SQL) is a composite metric calculated by the AQIA or its accredited CTEs.

| Dimension | Indicative Weight |
|-----------|------------------|
| Information veracity and accuracy | 25% |
| Thematic relevance to user context | 20% |
| Entity reputation quality (verified reviews) | 20% |
| Clarity, readability, and content accessibility | 15% |
| Ethical and regulatory compliance | 10% |
| Originality and informational added value | 10% |

### 6.2 Pricing Mechanics

The LLM-ADS pricing rests on a simple but powerful formula: **effective display cost is inversely proportional to Quality Score.** An advertiser with SQL 90/100 pays significantly less per display than an advertiser with SQL 50/100 for an equivalent position.

This mechanic creates structural pressure toward quality improvement and enables small high-quality entities to compete with larger-budget players.

### 6.3 SME and Startup Accessibility

An explicit objective of the protocol is not to reproduce the oligopolistic dynamics of current advertising platforms, where large companies dominate through budgetary power. LLM-ADS provides:
- Spending caps by category
- Priority access mechanisms for new entities
- Differentiated pricing by advertiser size
- Universal quality requirement as the common standard

## 7. Privacy and Data Protection

### 7.1 Privacy by Design Principle

The LLM-ADS protocol is designed from its origin according to the privacy by design principle as defined by the GDPR and its international equivalents. The system:
- Collects only data strictly necessary for its operation
- Retains no data beyond the active session unless explicit consent given
- Never exploits conversation content to generate inferences about user privacy

### 7.2 White Data

The "white data" concept is central to the protocol's privacy architecture:
- Voluntarily declared interests
- Membership in general thematic categories (no individual granularity)
- Anonymized session context signals
- Explicit filtering preferences

These data do not identify the user, do not reveal lifestyle habits, and do not constitute a behavioral profile in the legal sense.

**Fundamental Distinction:**
> LLM-ADS does not know WHO you are. It only knows WHAT interests you in this session. This distinction is not semantic â€” it is architectural: the system is technically incapable of establishing a link between a contextual signal and a real identity.

### 7.3 User Rights

The user has all rights recognized by applicable data protection regulations:
- Right of access to recorded preferences
- Right of rectification or deletion
- Right to immediate deactivation of the contextual window
- Right to portability of preferences between participating LLM operators
- Right to explanation of any content display reason
